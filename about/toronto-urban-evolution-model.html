Urban Evolution Observatory

The Toronto Urban Evolution Model

The Urban Evolution Observatory is built around the Toronto Urban Evolution Model, or TUEM.

TUEM starts from a simple intuition: cities change through processes that resemble evolution. New urban arrangements appear. Some spread. Some last. Some disappear. Some are copied imperfectly from one place to another. Some are reused for purposes their original builders did not imagine.

The model adapts Darwinian evolutionary theory to cities while treating cities as social and material places. Evolutionary concepts help us ask how urban forms, uses, and populations vary, persist, recombine, and travel.

The Basic Unit

TUEM defines the basic unit of urban evolution as the formeme.

A formeme is a piece of urban information about how to organize space for some groups and activities. A cul-de-sac, a main street, a shopping mall, a front porch, a zoning rule, a sidewalk cafe, or a neighborhood type can all carry formetic information. Each says, in effect: arrange space this way, for these kinds of uses, by or for these kinds of people.

A place is made up of many such pieces of information. TUEM calls the time-stamped bundle of formemes in a place its signature.

A signature has three linked parts:

  • Physical form: streets, buildings, parcels, infrastructure, and spatial layout.
  • Groups: residents, organizations, institutions, publics, and social populations.
  • Activities: businesses, services, routines, land uses, and everyday functions.

The key idea is that these parts evolve together. A street pattern matters because it organizes movement, access, visibility, exclusion, memory, and possibility. A building type matters because it carries assumptions about use and users. An activity matters because it can stabilize, recode, or transform the physical setting around it.

Why Evolutionary Theory Helps

Darwinian evolutionary theory gives TUEM a disciplined way to ask how cities change.

Variation

Urban life constantly produces variants. A new activity appears in an old building. A street type is copied in a new subdivision. A neighborhood form migrates from one city to another. A commercial strip adds new uses. A planning idea is translated into a different local setting. Variation is the source of new urban possibilities.

Selection

Some variants become more viable than others. A form may spread because it fits land markets, regulations, mobility systems, cultural expectations, climate, institutional power, or everyday routines. A form may fail because it is costly, fragile, isolated, restrictive, or poorly matched to the groups and activities around it. Selection is the process by which some urban arrangements gain ground.

Retention

Some arrangements persist. They persist through durable materials, repeated use, legal protection, habit, memory, infrastructure, investment, or institutional reproduction. A street grid can keep shaping a neighborhood long after its original uses change. A commercial corridor can preserve an activity logic across many waves of tenants. A neighborhood identity can survive through signals, stories, and repeated practices. Retention is how urban history keeps acting in the present.

From Model To Propositions

TUEM is useful because it generates propositions.

A proposition is a testable claim about urban evolution. It might ask whether physical form changes more slowly than activities, whether similar places can have different histories, whether inherited street structure shapes later recombination, or whether some activities appear before visible physical change.

The model gives each proposition a clear source:

  • Which part of the signature is changing?
  • Is the claim about physical form, groups, activities, or their relations?
  • Is the mechanism variation, selection, retention, recombination, migration, recoding, or trajectory?
  • What kind of evidence would support the claim?
  • What kind of evidence would force the claim to change?

This is why the Observatory is organized around propositions. The goal is to explore the space of the model, one claim at a time.

Why A Self-Evolving System

The original TUEM papers define the model. The Observatory turns that model into an ongoing research system.

A fixed paper can test a limited set of claims. A self-evolving system can keep asking what the model implies next.

Each cycle can refine an existing proposition, split a broad proposition into sharper claims, extend a proposition to a new scale or dataset, generate a novel proposition from an underexplored part of the model, identify a missing dataset, and report what the evidence changed.

The system begins with the existing proposition space of the model. It should keep exploring the grammar of TUEM: physical form, groups, activities, signatures, formemes, distance, variation, selection, retention, recombination, trajectory, scale, and time.

The aim is to let the model learn.

Original TUEM Papers

  1. Towards a Model of Urban Evolution: Part I: Context
    Introduces the need for an evolutionary model of urban life and develops the idea of the formeme.
  2. Towards a Model of Urban Evolution: Part II: Formal Model
    Defines the formal model of urban signatures, formemes, physical form, groups, activities, and distance.
  3. Towards a Model of Urban Evolution: Part III: Rules of Evolution
    Develops the model rules of variation, selection, retention, recombination, longevity, fidelity, fecundity, and trajectory.
  4. Towards a Model of Urban Evolution Part IV: Evolutionary Formetic Distance
    Applies TUEM to Yelp data to show how signatures and formetic distance can be used to compare places and trace change over time.
