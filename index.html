Urban Evolution Observatory

Evidence about how cities inherit the past and produce new forms of urban life.

The Observatory grows out of TUEM, the Toronto Urban Evolution Model. TUEM treats cities as changing combinations of physical form, groups, and activities. This site turns the model into propositions, tests those propositions against evidence, and revises them as the evidence changes.

What This Site Studies

Cities carry information about how space should be organized, who it is for, and what happens there. A street grid, a cul-de-sac, a shopping strip, a zoning rule, a front porch, or a sidewalk cafe can all carry such information.

TUEM calls these pieces of urban information formemes. A place has many formemes at once. Their time-stamped bundle is the place signature.

Physical Form

Streets, parcels, buildings, infrastructure, and spatial layout.

Groups

Residents, organizations, institutions, publics, and social populations.

Activities

Businesses, services, routines, land uses, and everyday urban functions.

Urban evolution happens as these parts become linked, persist, weaken, travel, or recombine.

How The Research Learns

The Observatory begins with propositions: claims about how cities evolve. A proposition might ask whether inherited street structure shapes later change, whether activities change before buildings do, or whether two places that look similar today arrived there through different histories.

Each report asks what the proposition would look like in data. It states what the evidence can observe, what it can only approximate, and what would count as support or challenge. The result then changes the proposition.

proposition -> evidence -> interpretation -> revision -> next proposition

A proposition can become stronger. It can become narrower. It can split into two claims. It can reveal a missing dataset. It can open a new direction for the model.

Current Evidence

The first public evidence set asks whether earlier movement in groups and activities predicts later physical recombination in the street network. The result is mixed: broad group and activity measures are weak, while inherited street structure is much stronger.
